You've decided to sell online. Now comes the question that stops more small business owners in their tracks than any pricing decision or product choice: which e-commerce platform do you actually use?

Shopify, WooCommerce, and Squarespace all promise to get your products in front of buyers. They all have free trials, glowing review pages, and success stories from brands that look nothing like your business. Choosing between them based on marketing copy alone is genuinely difficult.

This guide cuts through the noise. We compared the three platforms head-to-head on the factors that actually matter for small businesses — total cost, setup complexity, flexibility, SEO, payment processing, and how well they scale. By the end, you'll know which one fits your situation.

## Why Platform Choice Matters More Than You Think

Most small business owners treat e-commerce platform selection like picking a theme — something you can change later without much consequence. That's the wrong mental model.

Migrating e-commerce platforms is painful. You'll need to re-import product data, rebuild URLs (and manage SEO redirects), re-integrate payment processors, and potentially rebuild any custom features. Many businesses that switch platforms after a year or two absorb weeks of downtime, lost search rankings, and development costs that dwarf what they saved by picking the "cheaper" option upfront.

The right choice now saves you a messy migration later. The wrong choice costs more than you paid to avoid it.

## The Three Platforms at a Glance

Before diving into details, here's the honest summary:

**Shopify** is the most battle-tested dedicated e-commerce platform available. It's built specifically for selling online, handles technical complexity invisibly, and scales from five products to 50,000 without a rebuild. The tradeoff: it costs more at scale, takes a cut of every transaction unless you use Shopify Payments, and customization beyond templates requires either a developer or premium apps.

**WooCommerce** is a free plugin that turns a WordPress site into an online store. It's the most customizable option by a significant margin and costs less in platform fees — but you're responsible for hosting, security, updates, and plugin compatibility. It rewards technical users and punishes everyone else.

**Squarespace** is the easiest to launch. If you want your store live in an afternoon without touching a line of code, Squarespace gets you there. The tradeoff: it's the least flexible option for businesses that need advanced inventory management, complex shipping rules, or significant customization down the road.

## Cost Comparison: What You'll Actually Pay

Sticker prices mislead here. Here's a realistic view of what each platform costs for a small business doing $5K–$50K/month in revenue.

### Shopify

Shopify's plans run $39/month (Basic), $105/month (Shopify), and $399/month (Advanced). Most small businesses start on Basic.

On top of the plan fee, if you don't use Shopify Payments as your payment processor, Shopify charges transaction fees: 2% on Basic, 1% on Shopify, 0.5% on Advanced. At $10,000/month in revenue, that's $200/month extra on Basic — more than doubling your platform cost. Use Shopify Payments to eliminate this, though it's not available in all countries and its rates aren't always competitive.

Apps add up fast. Shopify's core product handles basics well, but you'll likely add apps for email marketing ($20–$50/month), reviews ($15–$30/month), advanced reporting ($30–$80/month), and upsells ($20–$40/month). A realistic total for a growing small business: $200–$400/month in platform, apps, and fees.

### WooCommerce

WooCommerce itself is free. What you pay for:

- **Hosting**: $15–$50/month for quality managed WordPress hosting (Kinsta, WP Engine, Cloudways)
- **Premium plugins**: $200–$600/year for plugins you'll likely need (advanced shipping, subscriptions, product bundles, payment gateways)
- **SSL certificate**: Usually included with hosting
- **Maintenance**: Either your time or $50–$150/month to someone else

For a small business handling basic operations, a realistic monthly cost is $50–$150/month — genuinely cheaper than Shopify. The catch: that number assumes you or someone on your team can handle updates, troubleshoot plugin conflicts, and respond when something breaks. If you're outsourcing all technical work, WooCommerce can cost more than Shopify in support fees.

### Squarespace

Squarespace's Commerce plans run $28/month (Basic) and $52/month (Advanced), billed annually. There are no transaction fees beyond what your payment processor charges.

What you won't find in Squarespace at this price: deep inventory management, advanced shipping logic, wholesale/B2B pricing, or the kind of extensibility Shopify and WooCommerce offer through apps and plugins. For businesses selling simple physical or digital products, Squarespace's price-to-value is excellent. For businesses with complex operations, you'll eventually hit walls.

## Ease of Setup and Use

This is where the platforms diverge most dramatically.

**Shopify** wins on guided setup. The onboarding flow is genuinely one of the best in the software industry. You can add products, connect a payment processor, and configure shipping in a few hours without reading documentation. The admin dashboard is clean, logical, and consistent across years of updates. Non-technical business owners routinely manage Shopify stores without ever needing developer help.

**Squarespace** comes close. The drag-and-drop editor is intuitive, templates are professionally designed, and the Commerce features are buried only slightly deeper than the general website features. If you've used Squarespace for a regular website, adding a store feels natural.

**WooCommerce** requires the most effort. You're starting with WordPress (which has its own learning curve), adding WooCommerce, configuring payment gateways, setting up tax rules, and potentially troubleshooting plugin conflicts — all before you add a single product. Managed WordPress hosts like Kinsta reduce the burden, but WooCommerce still demands more technical comfort than the alternatives.

## Customization and Flexibility

If you need your store to work in specific ways — complex product variants, subscription boxes, B2B pricing tiers, custom checkout flows, integrations with industry-specific software — this is where the platforms separate.

**WooCommerce** is the winner by a large margin. Because it's built on WordPress and the plugin ecosystem is enormous, there's almost nothing you can't do with WooCommerce. More importantly, because you have full access to the codebase, a developer can build anything your business needs. This is why WooCommerce powers more of the web's online stores than any other platform.

**Shopify** offers serious flexibility, especially through its app marketplace (8,000+ apps) and the Liquid templating language for theme customization. Most needs can be met with apps. The limitation: you're operating within Shopify's architecture. Some customizations that would be trivial in WooCommerce require expensive workarounds in Shopify.

**Squarespace** is the least flexible. It's a closed platform, and while it covers common use cases well, it's genuinely difficult to extend beyond what Squarespace built. If you find yourself wanting something Squarespace doesn't offer natively, your options are limited.

## SEO Capabilities

Getting organic search traffic to your store is a long game, and your platform either helps or hinders it.

**Shopify** handles SEO basics well out of the box — clean URLs, editable title tags and meta descriptions, automatic sitemap generation, canonical tags, and fast load speeds. The main SEO limitation: Shopify's URL structure is fixed (you can't remove `/products/` or `/collections/` from URLs), and blog functionality, while present, is less capable than WordPress.

**WooCommerce** pairs with WordPress, which is the gold standard for SEO. Plugins like Yoast SEO and Rank Math give you deep control over every on-page SEO element. URL structure is completely flexible. Blog content is where WordPress genuinely excels, and for businesses whose SEO strategy involves content marketing alongside their store, WooCommerce/WordPress is the strongest option.

**Squarespace** has improved its SEO tools significantly. Basic on-page SEO is well-covered. The limitation: less control over technical SEO elements, and the platform's JavaScript-heavy rendering can occasionally slow crawling on large sites. For most small businesses, Squarespace's SEO capabilities are sufficient.

## Payment Processing

Every platform integrates with major payment processors. The differences are in the details.

**Shopify Payments** (Shopify's built-in processor, powered by Stripe) eliminates transaction fees and supports Shop Pay — an accelerated checkout that meaningfully improves conversion rates. The tradeoff: Shopify Payments isn't available in every country, and if you use a third-party processor instead, you're paying transaction fees on top of processing fees.

**WooCommerce** works with virtually any payment processor through plugins. WooCommerce Payments (their native option, also Stripe-powered) is solid. Stripe, PayPal, Square, Authorize.net, and dozens of regional processors are all available. No platform-level transaction fees — you pay only the processor's rate.

**Squarespace** supports Stripe and PayPal on Commerce plans. Limited compared to the others, but covers most small business needs.

## Inventory Management

How well each platform handles products varies significantly at scale.

**Shopify** handles inventory management well for most businesses. Variants (size, color, material), product organization, collections, and inventory tracking are all solid. At high volume or with complex needs (kitting, manufacturing, multi-warehouse), you'll need third-party apps.

**WooCommerce** covers basics well and extends through plugins. If you need something unusual — bundles, subscriptions, product configurators, wholesale pricing — there's almost certainly a WooCommerce plugin for it.

**Squarespace** handles simple inventory (single-variant products in small catalogs) without issues. If you have hundreds of products with complex variants, you'll run into limitations.

## Shipping and Fulfillment

**Shopify** includes built-in calculated shipping rates from major carriers, connects to Shopify Shipping (with discounted UPS, USPS, and DHL rates), and integrates with third-party fulfillment providers including Amazon MCF. If you're shipping physical products at scale, Shopify's shipping tools are the most developed.

**WooCommerce** supports flat-rate, free, and calculated shipping natively, with plugins adding carrier rate calculation, dimensional weight, and third-party fulfillment integrations. WooCommerce Shipping offers discounted USPS and DHL rates similar to Shopify Shipping.

**Squarespace** offers basic carrier-calculated shipping and flat-rate options. Adequate for simple needs; limited for complex shipping logic.

## Mobile Commerce

Over half of e-commerce traffic is now mobile. All three platforms are mobile-responsive, but they're not equally optimized.

**Shopify** is excellent on mobile. Templates are designed mobile-first, and the Shop Pay checkout dramatically simplifies mobile purchases. Shopify also offers a free mobile app for store management.

**WooCommerce** depends heavily on your theme and how well it's optimized. A well-chosen theme performs beautifully on mobile; a poorly optimized one doesn't. Mobile performance is your responsibility to test and maintain.

**Squarespace** templates look great on mobile, and the platform handles responsive design automatically. Store management via mobile browser is functional but not as smooth as the Shopify app.

## When to Choose Each Platform

### Choose Shopify If…

- You want to focus on selling, not managing a platform
- You're doing significant volume and need proven infrastructure
- You want the best mobile commerce and checkout experience
- You plan to expand to selling on marketplaces (Amazon, Walmart, TikTok Shop, Instagram)
- You're not technical and don't want to hire someone who is

### Choose WooCommerce If…

- You already have a WordPress website and want to add e-commerce
- You need deep customization that Shopify's apps can't provide
- Content marketing is central to your SEO strategy
- You're comfortable with (or have access to) technical help
- You want maximum control over costs at scale

### Choose Squarespace If…

- You're launching a simple store with a limited product catalog
- Design quality and ease of launch are your top priorities
- You already use Squarespace for your business website
- You don't anticipate complex inventory or shipping needs

## The Migration Question

The most common mistake: choosing the easiest platform to launch on without considering where your business is headed in two years.

A boutique selling 20 products and processing $3,000/month will probably be fine on any of these platforms. A business that grows to 500 SKUs, two warehouse locations, and $80,000/month will need capabilities that Squarespace doesn't offer and will find a WooCommerce migration expensive and disruptive.

If you can see yourself scaling significantly, bias toward Shopify or WooCommerce even if the initial setup is harder. The migration cost you avoid is real.

## Practical Next Steps

Choosing a platform is a decision, not a project. Here's how to make it efficiently:

1. **Map your requirements first.** List your products (how many, what variants), shipping needs, payment methods, and any software you need to integrate (CRM, inventory management, accounting). This exercise often makes the right platform obvious.

2. **Try before you commit.** Shopify's 14-day free trial and Squarespace's 14-day trial let you test the actual admin experience, not just the marketing site. Start with the trial before paying for anything.

3. **Factor in your technical capacity.** If you don't have a developer relationship and aren't comfortable managing software, WooCommerce's lower cost can become a higher burden. Be honest about this.

4. **Ask about your payment processor.** If you already have a preferred processor, verify it integrates with your platform choice before committing.

5. **Think about SEO from day one.** If organic search is part of your growth strategy, your platform choice affects your trajectory. WordPress/WooCommerce gives you the most SEO flexibility; Shopify covers the fundamentals well; Squarespace is sufficient for most basic needs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which e-commerce platform is best for a beginner?

Shopify and Squarespace are both excellent for beginners. Shopify has better e-commerce-specific tools and scales further; Squarespace has a simpler overall interface if you also need a general business website. WooCommerce has a steeper learning curve and is better suited to users comfortable with WordPress.

### Can I switch platforms later if I outgrow my current one?

Yes, but it's not painless. Switching platforms requires migrating product data, customer records, and order history; rebuilding URLs with proper redirects to preserve SEO; and often rebuilding custom features. Plan your platform choice with your 2–3 year trajectory in mind to minimize the chance you'll need to migrate.

### Does Shopify really take a percentage of every sale?

Only if you use a third-party payment processor instead of Shopify Payments. Shopify charges 2% on the Basic plan, 1% on the Shopify plan, and 0.5% on Advanced. Using Shopify Payments eliminates these fees entirely. Shopify Payments is available in the US, Canada, UK, Australia, and most of Western Europe.

### Is WooCommerce really free?

WooCommerce the plugin is free, but running a WooCommerce store has real costs: quality WordPress hosting ($15–50/month), premium plugins for features like subscriptions or advanced shipping ($200–600/year), and maintenance time or fees. Total cost of ownership is comparable to Shopify at moderate volume, with potential savings at high volume if you're technically self-sufficient.

### Which platform has the best SEO?

WooCommerce on WordPress offers the most SEO flexibility, especially for content-driven strategies. Shopify handles core SEO requirements well and is adequate for most businesses. Squarespace covers basics and has improved significantly, but offers less control over technical SEO. For pure SEO capability, WordPress/WooCommerce leads.

The right e-commerce platform is the one your team can actually use, that handles your product complexity, and that grows with your revenue without forcing a disruptive migration in 18 months. For businesses where content marketing and customization matter most, WooCommerce earns its complexity. For businesses launching a simple storefront today, Squarespace gets you there fastest.
